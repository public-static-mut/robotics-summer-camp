Unaffiliated with any school, we welcome kids from all over the west side to have fun with robotics and compete on one of our award-winning robotics teams (SoCal Champs 2026!), regardless of what school they attend.

Why robotics?

Robotics competitions - especially FTC - have emerged as a super fun way for young people to get hands-on experience with technology and engineering: coding, 3d printing, CAD, motion planning, servo motors, PID control, computer vision, and more.

Robotics teams also foster real world skills like team building, project management, and collaborative problem-solving, skills that will be important no matter how AI shapes the world ahead. Like youth sports, robotics builds character and teaches kids to deal with adversity.

Competition Season Schedule:

Both FTC and FLL seasons kick off at the start of the school year. Each FTC team competes in at least 5 meets per season, plus one or two more if they advance in post-season play in March. FLL is a shorter season.

Reach out anytime because we're always recruiting and sometimes we have side competitions or can add a student mid-season. We also offer summer camps open to all, even if your child can't compete in the season due to scheduling or "geographical conflicts" (aka traffic!).

Practices: Each team usually meets at our workshop in Santa Monica twice per week, once on a weekday and once on a weekend. Some kids come in on off days to work on their subsystems, and we have occasional optional extra meetings approaching meets or on school holidays.

We're not anti-AI. It just doesn't really help.

This is a question we get all the time these days: "Why don't you just use AI to code the robot?" Because robotics is about iteration and refinement of mechanisms and code, AI just doesn't really help. And that's a good thing.

FTC robotics is about understanding your code and mechanisms and refining them over the course of months, not just generating endless quantities of new code. The robot has to interact with the physical field elements of this year's game, as well as the other robots. Even asking AI questions about FTC code tends to give wrong answers.
